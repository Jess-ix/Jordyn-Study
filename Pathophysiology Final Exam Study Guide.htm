Intro to Pathophysiology and Cellular Injuries
Concept Definition
Operational Definitions Anatomy: The study of the structure of the body
Physiology: The study of the function of the body
Pathology: The study of diseases/injuries
Pathophysiology: The study of deviations from the physiological norm that result in a diseased/injured state
4 Central Concepts Etiology: The cause of a disease
Pathogenesis: The mechanism/sequence of events that result in a diseased/injurious state
Morphology: Appearance of the disease
Functional Consequences: Clinical manifestation (signs and symptoms)
8 Common Functions of the Cells
  • Movement
  • Conductivity
  • Metabolic absorption
  • Secretion
  • Excretion
  • Respiration
  • Reproduction
  • Communication
Aetiologies of Cellular Injuries
  1. Metabolic
    • Impairment to normal metabolic function
    • Ex. hypoxia
  2. Physical
    • Physical agents that can directly injure cells
    • Ex. cold, heat, trauma, radiation, silica-exposure, electricity
  3. Chemical
    • Chemical agents that can directly injure cells
    • Drugs, alcohol, narcotics, pollutants, poisons
  4. Immunologic
  5. Genetic
  6. Infectious
Necrosis
  1. Coagulative Necrosis
    • Coagulations of cells with loss of nuclei and denatured proteins
    • Ex. infarct in heart and kidneys
  2. Liquefactive necrosis
    • Hydrolytic enzymes from lysosomes which liquify tissues
    • Ex. Ischemia in the brain
  3. Fat Necrosis
    • Occurs in adipose tissues when there is a release of lipases from dead cells
    • Ex. Acute pancreatitis, some breast malignancies
  4. Caseous Necrosis
    • Combo of liquefactive and coagulative necrosis
    • Ex. tuberculosis
  5. Gangrenous Necrosis
    • Coagulative necrosis plus liquefactive necrosis
    • Ex. typically affects diabetics and limbs
Inflammation
Concept Details
Aetiologies of Inflammation
  • Infection
  • Physical agents
  • Trauma
  • Anoxia/hypoxia
  • Metabolic Injury
  • Immunological reaction
Acute vs Chronic Inflammation Acute Inflammation
  • Short duration
  • Neutrophils involved
  • Ex. cough for 1 day
Chronic Inflammation
  • Long (days/years)
  • Lymphocytes involved
  • Cough for 3 months
Acute Inflammation Pathogenesis
  1. Injury
  2. Vasodilation
  3. Increased vascular permeability
  4. Margination
  5. Rolling
  6. Adhesion
  7. Transmitigation
  8. Chemotaxis
  9. Leukocyte activation
  10. Phagocytosis

1-3 occur at the vascular level
4-10 occurs at the cellular level
4 Morphological Patterns of acute inflammation Serous
  • Accumulation of fluid and injury under the surface of the epithelium
  • Ex. sunburn leading to blister
Fibrinous
  • Damage of microvasculature
  • Ex. damage to meninges, pericardium, etc.
Suppurative
  • Accumulation of pus caused by bacterial infection
Ulcer
  • Defect of the surface of an organ with an epithelial lining
  • Ex. ulcerative colitis, peptic ulcers

Cardiovascular System
Concept Details
Heart Anatomy Layers
  1. Fibrous pericardium
  2. Serous pericardium
  3. Epicardium
  4. Myocardium
  5. Endocardium

Last 3 compose the heart wall
Heart anatomy chambers
  1. Right atrium
  2. Right ventricle
  3. Left atrium
  4. Left ventricle
Circulation- blood flow
  1. Deoxygenated blood enters heart via the superior and inferior vena cava, enters into the right atria to the right ventricle via the atrioventricular valve
  2. Exits to the pulmonary system via the pulmonary arteries
  3. Oxygenated blood returns to the heart via the pulmonary veins, enters into the left atria, to the left ventricle via the mitral valve
  4. Exits to systemic circulation via the aorta
Pathologies
Atherosclerosis
Etiology
  • Multifaceted
  • Family history, smoking, hypertension, diabetes
Pathogenesis
  • Damaged epithelium
  • Macrophages ingest lipids, foam cells fatty streak
  • Fibrous lesion leads to complicated lesion
Clinical Manifestations
  • Pre-cardiac event
  • Tests may show increased LDL cholesterol and decreased HDL cholesterol
  • Increased inflammatory/thrombosis markers in serum
  • No symptoms until severe atherosclerosis
  • Some symptoms include hypertensive, lethargy, loss of appetite/nausea/malaise, peripheral edema
Treatment
  • ECG, ACE inhibitors, beta blockers, anti-hypertensive drugs, anti-clotting, calcium channel blockers
Myocardial Infarction
Etiology
  • Most commonly atherosclerosis
  • But also drug use, ventricular hypertrophy, primary coronary vasospasm, hypoxemia
Pathogenesis
  • Occlusion/obstruction of coronary arteries, lead to myocardial ischemia, hypoxia, coronary thrombosis, myocardial necrosis
Clinical Manifestations

Males:

  • Angina
  • Dyspnea
  • Radiating shoulder pain
  • Diaphoresis
  • Sweating
  • Nausea
  • Cyanosis

Females:

  • Angina
  • Dyspnea
  • Radiating shoulder pain
  • Diaphoresis
  • Sweating
  • Nausea
  • Cyanosis
  • General malaise
Treatments
  • ECG, nitrates, anti-coagulants, supplemental O2, NSTEMI, angiography, CABG, post ACS therapy
Respiratory System
Concept Details
Respiratory System Components

Comprised of upper/lower respiratory tract

Upper respiratory tract
  • Nose
  • Nasal Cavity
  • Sinuses
  • Pharynx
  • Larynx
Lower Respiratory Tract
  • Trachea
  • Bronchial Tree
  • Lungs
Lung Anatomy
  • Right lung has three lobes
  • Left lung has 2 lobes (because of the heart)
Anatomy of the Nose
  • Exterior protruding structure of the face that acts as an air filter
  • Paranasal sinuses are the internal hollow spaces of the nasal passages that function with the nose to humidify/warm air + smell
Oral Cavity
  • Space between the palate/tongue leading to the pharynx
  • Mouth is the secondary opening in the upper respiratory tract
  • Mouth/oral cavities function in respiratory system= breathing + air supplementation
Anatomy of the Pharynx
  • A muscular tube that begins at the skull, C6 and functions to connect the nasal/oral cavities to the larynx
  1. Nasopharynx
  2. Oropharynx
  3. Laryngopharynx
Anatomy of the larynx
  • A connection between the upper/lower airways comprised of endolarynx and cartilaginous structures
  • The larynx aids in vocal manifestations and accessory muscles that aid in swallowing and ventilation
Anatomy of the trachea
  • Cartilaginous ringed structure that connects the larynx and bronchi to allow air to pass into the lungs
Anatomy Alveoli
  • Small, highly vascularized balloon like structures within the lungs
  • Site of gas exchange
Respiratory System Physiology Physical Process
  • 2 parts
Inspiration Expiration
  • During inspiration, diaphragm contracts
  • During expiration, diaphragm relaxes
Pathologies
Pneumothorax
  • Build up of air outside the lung within the pleural cavity
Etiology
  • Trauma/lung disease/ in some cases no underlying lung disease
Treatment
  • Chest tube- drain air which will allow the lung to expand
Pleural Effusion
  • Abnormal buildup of fluid outside of lungs in pleural cavity
  • Restricts lungs from fully expanding
Etiology
  • Cancer, but also from congestive heart failure
Treatment
  • Thoracocentesis
  • Antibiotics
COPD
  • Characterized by persistent airflow limitation/inflammation
Etiology
  • Tobacco smoke exposure
  • Occupational hazards
  • Air pollution and genetics
Pathogenesis
  • Prolonged inflammatory response, inhaled injurious agents, over time leads to chronic airflow limitation
Clinical Manifestations
  • Chronic cough
  • Dyspnea
  • Mucus
  • Wheezing
  • Chest tightness
  • Fatigue
  • Barrel chested
Treatments
  • Decrease risk factor exposure
  • Supplementary O2, bronchodilators, nebulizers/inhaled steroids

GI System Pathophysiology
Gastrointestinal Reflux Disorder
  • Reflux of gastric acid from the stomach into the distal aspect of the esophagus, leading to esophagitis
Etiology
  • Conditions that decrease LES tone or increase abdominal pressure
Pathogenesis
  • Reflux of stomach acid/gastric juices lead to mucosal injury
Clinical manifestations
  • Hyperemia, eosinophils recruited to squamous mucosa of esophagus
  • Heartburn
  • Dysphagia
  • Sour taste in mouth
  • Chest pain
Treatments
  • Proton-pump inhibitors
  • H2 histamine receptor antagonists
Peptic Ulcer Disease
  • Break or ulceration in the protective mucosal lining of the lower esophagus, stomach or duodenum
Etiology
  • Prolonged NSAID use
  • H. pylori infection
Clinical Manifestations
  • Depends on gastric vs duodenal ulcers
  • Tend to be chronic
  • Cause anorexia, vomiting and weight loss
Treatments
  • Anti-acids
  • Drugs inhibiting acid secretion
  • H. pylori eradication
  • Surgery
Inflammatory Bowel Diseases
  • Group of conditions that result in an inflammation of the large intestine
Epidemiology
  • Individuals aged 15-35
  • Chronic diseases characterized by exacerbations and periods of remission
  • May result in extraintestinal problems such as primary sclerosing cholangitis, arthritis, uveitis/episcleritis, erythema, nodosum/pyoderma gangrenosum and oral manifestations
Pathogenesis
  • Combination of abnormal immunological reaction to the gut flora, increase in t-cell activation, decrease t-reg control

  • Impaired protein synthesis
  • Hepatocellular carcinomas
  • Jaundice
Hepatitis
Hepatitis A
  • Spread via fecal-oral route
  • Mild/asymptomatic
  • Epidemics are common
  • Pre-exposure vaccine
Hepatitis B
  • Spread via blood/bodily fluids route
  • At risk populations: drug users, healthcare workers, hemodialysis patients, high risk sex
Hepatitis C
  • Spread via blood/bodily fluids
  • Most at risk- IV drug users, high risk sex
  • No vaccination, just treatment post-exposure
Hepatitis D
  • Spread via blood/bodily fluids
  • Defective virus can only replicate in the presence of hepatitis B
  • Pre-exposure vaccine

Pathologies
Renal Failure
  • 1 or both kidneys is not functioning at full capacity
  • Classified as either acute or chronic
Aetiologies
  1. Pre-renal/systemic
    • Shock
    • Dehydration
    • Hemorrhage
    • Leads to reduced perfusion of the kidneys and ultimately renal failure
  2. Renal
    • Inflammation
    • Toxins
    • Drug-use
    • Infection
    • Renal ischemia
  3. Post-renal
    • Urinary tract obstruction
    • Leads to reduced capacity/blockage of urine elimination
Consequences
  • Edema
  • Electrolyte
  • Metabolic acidosis
  • Azotemia
  • Anemia
  • Osteodystrophy
  • Anemia often occurs with renal failure
Treatments
  • Dietary management
  • Medical interventions including EP, antihypertensives, diuretics, dialysis and renal transplantation
Renal Osteodystrophy
  • Bone/skeletal abnormalities that occur in individuals that suffer from chronic kidney disease/end stage renal disease
Etiology
  • Clinical consequence of chronic kidney disease
Pathophysiology
  • Normal kidneys produce calcitriol
  • In renal failure, decrease calcitriol, thyroid hyperplasia and impaired bone mineralization and increased bone reabsorption
